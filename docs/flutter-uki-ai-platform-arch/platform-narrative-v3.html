01
Vision
Why this platform exists

Artificial intelligence is moving from experimentation to operational infrastructure. Competitors are deploying AI-powered workflows that reduce cost, accelerate decisions, and create customer experiences that were previously impossible at scale. The question is no longer whether to invest in AI — it is whether to build the capability to do so systematically, or to remain dependent on ad-hoc point solutions that cannot scale, cannot be governed, and cannot be trusted in regulated markets.

Flutter operates across a portfolio of regulated betting brands — PokerStars, Paddy Power, Betfair, Sky Betting & Gaming — each subject to distinct regulatory obligations around Responsible Gambling, Anti-Money Laundering, data protection, and consumer fairness. Deploying AI capabilities in this environment without a shared governance foundation creates compounding risk: fragmented safety controls, unprovable audit trails, identity models that break at brand boundaries, and no consistent answer to the question a regulator will eventually ask — how do you know your AI did what you said it did?

A governed, multi-tenant AI control plane that enforces safety, identity, and audit invariants unconditionally across every AI capability deployed within the organisation — regardless of which team built it, which brand runs it, or which model powers it.

The platform is not a productivity tool or an AI feature set. It is a control plane. It provides a shared, governed, reusable foundation for AI across every brand and function — purpose-built for enterprise scale, multi-brand operation, and the regulatory requirements of a licensed gaming operator. It absorbs the hard, shared problems — security, compliance, cost control, identity, data governance — so that product teams spend their time building agents that deliver value, not re-solving infrastructure.

Platform owns Layers 1–3. Product and brand teams own Layers 4–5. This is not a centralisation of AI; it is a centralisation of the infrastructure that makes AI safe and scalable.

02
Why Now
The cost of not building

Without a shared platform, each team builds their own guardrails, their own data pipelines, their own observability. The cost is paid in duplicated effort, inconsistent safety controls, and an inability to share capability across brands. A guardrail failure in one brand that could have been caught by a platform-level control is not a technical risk — it is a regulatory and reputational risk.

03
Architecture
Five layers, three cross-cutting pillars

The platform is structured as five technology layers from authoring surface to data plane, with three pillars — Governance, Security, Observability — enforced vertically across all of them. No layer operates outside the pillars. Every AI capability deployed on the platform passes through all five layers on every request.

Authoring produces a Capability Definition — the governed artefact that every other layer reasons about. Once published through the Registry gate, the runtime layers execute it within the constraints declared at authoring time. The pillars are not add-ons; they are the reason the layers are structured the way they are.

04
How
Four strategic pillars

The platform is not just a technology stack — it is an opinionated set of choices about how enterprise AI should be built in a regulated, multi-brand organisation. Four pillars guide every architecture and build decision.

AWS-Native Foundation
Built on AWS managed services — Bedrock for LLM inference, AgentCore for agent hosting, Step Functions for durable workflow execution, and OpenSearch for vector retrieval. AWS-native means reduced operational burden, stronger security posture, and alignment with existing infrastructure investment. Third-party tools are adopted only where AWS has a genuine gap.
Standards-Based Interoperability
Agents and tools are integrated using open standards — MCP (Model Context Protocol) for tool connectivity and A2A for agent-to-agent communication. Capabilities built today are not locked to a single vendor or framework. Agents can be composed and shared across brands without bespoke integration work.
Non-Negotiable Guardrails
Content safety, PII detection, data loss prevention, and prompt injection protection are enforced at the gateway level — not optional, not bypassable, and fully auditable. Every LLM invocation is logged. Every Audit Record is written synchronously. This is the foundation of operating AI in a UKGC-regulated environment.
Agent Marketplace
Agents built on the platform are discoverable, rated, versioned, and shareable across brands. A compliance agent built for one brand can be adopted by another in hours, not months. The marketplace model transforms AI capability from a cost centre into a shared asset with network effects across the portfolio.
05
Delivery
The journey of deploying an agent

The phases are not abstract capability milestones — they describe a concrete, progressive expansion of what a team can build and how far the platform carries them. Follow a single agent from idea to production to see what each phase unlocks.

The example: a Paddy Power Customer Service agent that answers account queries, retrieves betting history, and — at higher risk tiers — applies responsible gambling interventions.

1
Phase 1 — Foundation: the first governed agent
A developer on the Paddy Power team authenticates via Okta → IAM Identity Center and receives an STS session with brand and role tags. They author a Capability Definition: a system prompt, an R0 Risk Tier declaration (read-only), and tool bindings to the customer knowledge base. The definition passes the Registry publish gate — Safety Envelope validated, tool bindings inspected, evaluation suite run by Promptfoo. The agent is deployed to AgentCore. On first invocation, the request flows through the LLM Gateway (quota checked, cost attributed), Bedrock Guardrails (PII redacted, injection blocked), and RAG retrieval (brand-isolated by session tag). An Audit Record is committed to S3. The team has a governed, observable, brand-isolated R0 agent in production — identity to audit trail, end-to-end.
2
Phase 2 — Platform Maturity: the agent writes and waits for humans
The same team now wants the agent to update a customer's communication preferences — an R2 action (customer-adjacent write). They version the Capability Definition to R2, add a Workflow Contract (idempotency key, compensation logic), and declare a HITL gate before the write executes. The platform enforces the declaration: Step Functions manages the durable workflow, the HITL service pauses execution and routes the approval to a human operator, and the Audit Record switches to fail-closed semantics — the write cannot proceed if the audit pipeline is unavailable. The agent is now published to the marketplace — the Betfair team discovers it, forks the definition, rebinds it to their own knowledge base and brand tags, and deploys their own instance. One agent design, two governed deployments, no integration work.
3
Phase 3 — Ecosystem: agents compose and collaborate
The Customer Service agent now needs to trigger a Responsible Gambling assessment — an R3 action (regulated write). Rather than embedding that logic, it invokes a specialist RG Assessment agent via A2A protocol. The orchestrating agent delegates, the specialist executes under its own Capability Definition and Safety Envelope, and both agents' Audit Records are correlated by execution ID. Promptfoo regression tests run as PR gates on every definition change — the evaluation framework is integrated to CI/CD, not a manual step. The marketplace now has network effects: agents built by one team are consumed, rated, and improved by others across brands.
→
2027+ Vision — not in scope
Autonomous agent negotiation, coalition formation, predictive orchestration. Architectural decisions in Phases 1–3 must not preclude this direction, but it is not a current deliverable. The platform's standards-based integration layer (MCP + A2A) and the Capability Registry's versioned contract model are the foundations that make this direction possible without re-platforming.
06
Request Flow
What happens on every Execution

Every AI capability invocation follows the same governed path — from the surface that called it, through identity establishment, into the runtime, through safety enforcement, out to tool calls, and back with a synchronously committed Audit Record. The steps below describe that path for a typical R1 or R2 Execution. R3 adds a write-ahead Audit Record commitment and a HITL gate before any state-changing tool call executes.

1
Identity established
The calling surface authenticates through Okta, federated via IAM Identity Center (SAML 2.0). IAM Identity Center issues an STS session with injected session tags — tenant_id, brand, role, use-case. This is the Identity Context for the Execution. Raw user credentials stop here — they do not flow past this boundary. The Identity Context is immutable for the duration of the session.
2
Capability Definition resolved
AgentCore loads the Published Capability Definition from the Registry — the specific version, its Safety Envelope, Risk Tier, Execution Model declaration, and permitted Tool Bindings. Draft or Review-state definitions are rejected. The ABAC check against Identity Context and Tool Bindings runs here — if the caller's session tags do not permit the tool set declared in the definition, the request is rejected before any model call is made.
3
Agent loop executes
The agent's perceive-reason-act loop runs within AgentCore. The LLM call is routed through the LLM Gateway, which enforces per-tenant quota, checks prompt cache, selects the provider, and attributes cost. Bedrock Guardrails evaluates every prompt and response against the Safety Envelope — PII detection, injection blocking, denied topic enforcement, output constraints. Each LLM call produces a Langfuse span. For Coordination-scope capabilities, the orchestrating agent invokes specialist capabilities via the same path recursively.
4
Tool calls execute under Identity Context
When the agent calls a tool, the MCP Gateway performs an RFC 8693 token exchange — the session-scoped IAM credential is transformed into a tool-scoped token that carries the Identity Context forward. The downstream tool receives a credential that proves who is calling and on behalf of which tenant — it cannot be impersonated and cannot exceed the ABAC permissions granted at session start. Tool calls are recorded as spans in the Langfuse trace and as entries in the Audit Record.
5
Audit Record committed
On Execution close, the Audit Record is written synchronously to S3. It contains: the Identity Context, the Capability Definition version and SHA-256, the Safety Envelope version, every tool call and its response hash, all guardrail decisions, token counts, and attributed cost. For R0/R1 a pipeline outage triggers fail-open with alerting. For R2/R3 the Execution halts — the record must commit before the response is returned. For R3 the record is committed before any state-changing tool call executes, not after.
6
Observability updated
CloudWatch metrics increment — error counts, latency, guardrail blocks, LLM Gateway quota consumption. The Langfuse trace is closed with the full span tree. Cost is posted to the per-tenant spend ledger and evaluated against the configured budget cap. If the cap is breached the circuit breaker opens, CloudWatch raises an alarm, and PagerDuty notifies the on-call engineer. For R2/R3 Process-scope executions, the Step Functions execution record is the persistent state of the workflow — queryable independently of the Langfuse trace.

Domain Model
The platform's ubiquitous language

Eight entities form the shared vocabulary across all architecture documents and views. Every component in the platform exists to store, enforce, or execute something described by one of these entities. When a term appears in a view or a design decision, it refers to one of these.

Entity When One-liner
Capability Definition Design-time The immutable, versioned specification of an AI capability — the platform's fundamental unit of management. All governance derives from this artefact.
Safety Envelope Design-time A versioned policy component of the Capability Definition. Declares permitted content scope, PII handling, injection controls, output constraints. Immutable on publish. Enforced by Guardrails at runtime but cannot be changed by it.
Risk Tier Design-time R0–R3 classification of what a Capability is permitted to affect. Drives every governance control proportionally — evaluation rigour, HITL, audit retention, fail-open vs fail-closed semantics. See Section 8.
Execution Model Design-time A declared field on the Capability Definition — Reasoning (always), Coordination (optional, for orchestration), Process (required for R2/R3). Registry-visible; not an implementation detail hidden in code.
Execution Runtime A runtime instance of a Published Capability Definition processing a request. Traces to exactly one Definition version, one Identity Context, and produces one Audit Record. The atomic unit of platform accountability.
Identity Context Runtime The propagated security principal for an Execution — established once at session start, immutable for its duration. Carries tenant, brand, role, and use-case. Every tool call and ABAC decision is a function of it.
Audit Record Runtime The immutable output of every Execution. Written synchronously — not best-effort logging. Contains identity, capability version, safety state, all tool calls, guardrail traces, cost. Retention driven by Risk Tier.
Workflow Contract Runtime · R2/R3 Required for R2/R3 Capabilities. Defines correlation, idempotency, compensation semantics, and ABAC at step boundaries. The Registry will not publish R2/R3 without one.

Risk Tier
Governance proportional to risk

Risk Tier is the single most consequential classification a team makes when defining a capability. Every governance control in the platform — how rigorous the evaluation gate is, whether HITL is required, how long audit records are retained, whether the platform fails open or closed on an outage — is derived from it. Over-classifying is safe but inefficient. Under-classifying is a governance failure.

The R0/R1 boundary is structurally enforced — the Registry inspects Tool Bindings and rejects write-capable bindings on declared R0 capabilities. The R1/R2 boundary is a deliberate declaration decision that cannot be made by accident.

Tier Definition Workflow Contract HITL Audit on outage
R0 Read-OnlyNo write tool bindings. Retrieves and responds. Cannot modify any system of record. Structurally enforced at publish. Not required Not required Fail-open
R1 Operational WriteWrites to internal systems only. Subject must be non-customer. No regulatory reporting obligation on the target system. Not required Optional Fail-open
R2 Customer-AdjacentWrites where subject is a customer or data carries regulatory significance. A wrong write can harm a customer or generate a regulatory question. Mandatory Required Fail-closed
R3 RegulatedWrites with direct regulatory obligation — RG interventions, AML flags, financial transactions. Wrong writes are reportable events. Write-ahead audit before any tool call. Mandatory + compensation Mandatory · no waiver Fail-closed · write-ahead

Non-Goals
What the platform does not do

Clarity about what the platform does not own matters as much as what it does. These boundaries prevent teams from pushing responsibility onto the platform and prevent the platform from over-reaching into decisions that belong to the teams building on it.

×
Does not replace application logic. The platform governs how AI capabilities are deployed and executed. It does not define the business logic within them. A Responsible Gambling decision engine built on the platform owns its own decisioning logic — the platform ensures that logic runs governed, not that it is correct.
×
Does not own Risk Tier classification. Risk Tier is a team decision made at authoring time. The platform validates and enforces the declared tier — it does not determine which tier a given capability should be. A team that misclassifies R2 work as R1 has made a governance failure the platform cannot prevent, only make harder to do accidentally.
×
Does not make regulatory decisions. The platform enforces the conditions under which AI capabilities are permitted to operate. It does not determine whether a specific AI output satisfies a regulatory obligation. That remains a human and legal function. The platform's contribution is ensuring every Execution is traceable, auditable, and governed — not that every output is compliant.
×
Does not guarantee output quality. Guardrails enforce the Safety Envelope and the evaluation gate assesses quality at publish time. Neither guarantees correct or high-quality outputs at runtime. Quality monitoring and human feedback loops are the responsibility of the teams operating capabilities on the platform.
×
Does not constrain model choice unnecessarily. The LLM Gateway routes to multiple providers and does not mandate a single model. Teams declare permitted model selection in their Capability Definition — the platform enforces the declared boundary but does not impose one beyond what Risk Tier and Safety Envelope require.
